# Term Rewriting - Reading Notes

Background reading to get more fluent in term-rewriting theory and see how
other implementations solved problems Ruleset Notebook will eventually hit
(strategies, confluence, termination, efficient matching). Organized as
notes-to-self, not a literature review - each entry says what it's about and
why it's relevant here.

## 1. Core vocabulary (so the rest of this doc is legible)

- **Signature** - the set of function/constant symbols and their arities.
  Ruleset Notebook's `Func`/`Const` (`app.py`) are exactly this.
- **Substitution** - a mapping from variables to terms; `env` in `match`/
  `substitute` in `app.py` is a substitution.
- **Redex** ("reducible expression") - a subterm that matches some rule's
  LHS and can be rewritten.
- **Rewrite step / relation** - `t -> t'` if `t'` is obtained from `t` by
  replacing one redex with its instantiated RHS. `evaluate_with_trace`'s
  trace lines are literally a sequence of rewrite steps.
- **Normal form** - a term with no redexes (nothing left to rewrite).
  Ruleset Notebook's `"normal form"` status.
- **Confluence** - if `t` can reach both `t1` and `t2` by different rewrite
  paths, there's always some `t3` both can reach. Confluent + terminating =>
  every term has a *unique* normal form, independent of which redex you pick
  first. Ruleset Notebook sidesteps needing this by fixing one deterministic
  strategy (ordered rules, leftmost/root match wins) instead of proving
  confluence - see `plan.md`'s "Explicitly deferred" list, which calls out
  "Proving termination or confluence."
- **Church–Rosser property** - equivalent formulation of confluence in terms
  of the equational theory generated by the rules (`t1 =E t2` iff they have
  a common reduct). Comes up constantly in the literature as "CR."
- **Termination (strong normalization)** - no infinite rewrite sequence
  exists. Undecidable in general; Ruleset Notebook works around it
  pragmatically with a step limit (`max_steps`) rather than proving
  termination.
- **Critical pair** - the classic tool for *checking* confluence for
  terminating systems: overlap two rule LHSs against each other (unify a
  subterm of one LHS with the other LHS) and see if the two possible
  rewrites can still be reconciled. Central to the Knuth–Bendix method
  below.
- **Rewriting strategy** - the policy for choosing *which* redex to reduce
  when several exist: innermost (reduce arguments before the surrounding
  term - closest to call-by-value), outermost (closest to call-by-name/lazy
  evaluation), leftmost variants of each, or fully non-deterministic
  ("parallel"/"maximal" strategies used in equality saturation, Section 5).
  Ruleset Notebook currently has *two* strategies in the same codebase and
  should pick one deliberately - see `compiler-plan.md` Section 11: `app.py`'s
  bare `evaluate()` is innermost (args reduced before root), while
  `evaluate_with_trace()` (what the GUI actually uses) only ever rewrites at
  the root.
- **Matching vs. unification** - matching (used here) instantiates variables
  in the *pattern* only, subject stays fixed (`match(pattern, subject)`).
  Unification instantiates variables on both sides to make two terms equal;
  needed for narrowing (Section 2) and for computing critical pairs.
- **Conditional rewriting** - rules with a side condition,
  `lhs -> rhs if cond`. Ruleset Notebook's `when ?y > 0` guards are a
  deliberately restricted case of this (no recursive rewriting inside the
  condition, just a comparison against a literal).

## 2. Foundational texts

- **Baader & Nipkow, *Term Rewriting and All That* (Cambridge University
  Press, 1998).** The standard first textbook. Covers abstract reduction
  systems, confluence/Church–Rosser, termination orderings (recursive path
  order, Knuth–Bendix order), Knuth–Bendix completion, and a chapter on
  unification. Directly useful chapters for this project: abstract
  reduction systems (formalizes exactly the `match`/`substitute`/rewrite-step
  triple already in `app.py`) and the termination-ordering chapter (relevant
  if Ruleset Notebook ever wants to warn "this ruleset looks non-terminating"
  instead of just hitting the step limit).
  [website](https://www21.in.tum.de/~nipkow/TRaAT/) [g books](https://books.google.com/books/about/Term_Rewriting_and_All_That.html?id=N7BvXVUCQk8C)
- **Klop, "Term Rewriting Systems," in *Handbook of Logic in Computer
  Science*, vol. 2 (Oxford University Press, 1992).** The classic long-form
  survey/reference chapter predating Baader & Nipkow; denser but more
  comprehensive (orthogonality, infinite rewriting, higher-order extensions
  in later handbook chapters by Klop and others).
  [acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/162552.162559)
- **Terese (M. Bezem, J.W. Klop, R. de Vrijer, eds.), *Term Rewriting
  Systems* (Cambridge University Press, 2003).** The "big book" - treats
  first-order, many-sorted, and higher-order rewriting uniformly. Good
  reference once past the Baader & Nipkow basics; not a first read.
  [g books](https://www.google.com/books/edition/Term_Rewriting_Systems/7QQ5u-4tRUkC)
- **Dershowitz & Jouannaud, "Rewrite Systems," in *Handbook of Theoretical
  Computer Science*, vol. B (1990).** Another widely cited survey chapter,
  strong on termination orderings and completion; a common alternative
  entry point to Klop's chapter.
  [acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/114891.114897)
- **Knuth & Bendix, "Simple Word Problems in Universal Algebras" (1970).**
  The original paper introducing what's now called Knuth–Bendix completion:
  given a set of equations, try to turn them into a confluent, terminating
  rewrite system by orienting equations into rules and resolving critical
  pairs by adding new rules. This is the historical root of "compile
  equations into a good rewrite system," which is conceptually close to
  what `compiler-plan.md` is doing at a much smaller scale (compile a fixed
  rewrite system into C rather than derive the rewrite system itself).
  [webpdf](https://www.cs.tufts.edu/~nr/cs257/archive/don-knuth/knuth-bendix.pdf) 

## 3. Strategies, pattern-match compilation, and efficient matching

- **Maranget, "Compiling Pattern Matching to Good Decision Trees" (ML
  Workshop, 2008).** Directly relevant to `compiler-plan.md`'s Section 5
  "true per-rule codegen" stretch goal: how functional-language compilers
  (OCaml, and the technique is used across the ML/Haskell family) turn a
  sequence of pattern-matching clauses into an efficient decision tree
  instead of a naive linear scan. Ruleset Notebook's rule list is exactly
  such a clause sequence (`for rule in rules: match(...)`), so this is the
  paper to read before attempting anything smarter than linear rule-by-rule
  matching.
  [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1411304.1411311) [webpdf](http://moscova.inria.fr/~maranget/papers/ml05e-maranget.pdf)
- **Kirchner & Kirchner et al. on the RETE algorithm (Forgy, "Rete: A Fast
  Algorithm for the Many Pattern/Many Object Pattern Match Problem," 1982).**
  Not a term-rewriting-to-normal-form algorithm at all - Rete is for
  *production rule systems* (Drools, CLIPS, OPS5) where many rules match
  against a large, changing set of facts and you want incremental
  re-matching, not single-term reduction. Worth reading specifically to
  understand *why it doesn't apply here*: Ruleset Notebook rewrites one term
  to its normal form, not a growing/shrinking working memory of facts.
  Useful contrast when explaining to someone why "just use Rete" isn't the
  right answer for this project.
  [acm](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1016/0004-3702%2882%2990020-0)
- **AC (associative-commutative) matching.** Not needed for the current demo
  grammar (no built-in associative/commutative operators), but worth knowing
  the name: matching modulo AC (e.g. treating `+` as associative and
  commutative so `add(x, y)` matches `add(y, x)`) is a well-studied, harder
  variant of matching used by Maude and most industrial-strength rewriting
  engines. Flag as a deliberately deferred feature if it ever comes up,
  the same way `plan.md` already defers "alternative rewrite strategies."
  [zuckers site](https://www.philipzucker.com/relational-ac-matching/)

## 4. Popular implementations worth knowing

| System | What it is | Why it's worth a look |
|---|---|---|
| **Maude** (SRI/UIUC, Clavel et al.) | A high-performance implementation of *rewriting logic*, with built-in AC/C matching, reflection, and both equational and "rewrite rule" (non-deterministic, concurrent) layers. | The closest thing to a production-grade analog of what Ruleset Notebook is a toy version of. Its manual's discussion of "equations vs. rules" (equations = deterministic, confluent, terminating, evaluated to normal form; rules = possibly non-deterministic, model concurrent/nondeterministic systems) is a clean way to think about what Ruleset Notebook currently supports (equations only, one deterministic strategy). |
| **Stratego/XT** (Visser et al., part of the Meta-Environment / now Spoofax) | A *strategic* term-rewriting language: instead of one fixed global strategy, you write small combinators (`try`, `repeat`, `<+`, sequencing) to control exactly where and in what order rewrites apply. | Directly relevant if Ruleset Notebook ever wants configurable strategies beyond "ordered rules, root-only, first match wins." Stratego's separation of *rules* (what to rewrite) from *strategy* (when/where) is a good target architecture to borrow from. |
| **OBJ3 / CafeOBJ / Elan** | Earlier algebraic specification / rewriting languages in the same lineage as Maude (OBJ came first; CafeOBJ and Elan are contemporaries with different strategy/reflection designs). | Mostly historical interest now, but OBJ3's "order-sorted" term algebra (types/sorts constrain which terms are well-formed) is worth skimming if Ruleset Notebook ever wants a type system beyond "well-formed by arity." |
| **ASF+SDF / Rascal** (CWI, Meta-Environment lineage) | Term rewriting tightly integrated with a parser generator, aimed at language engineering (build a parser and a rewrite-based transformer for the same grammar together). | Relevant to the "convert inputs to YAML" step conceptually - ASF+SDF's whole pitch is that syntax definition and rewriting should share one toolchain, which is close in spirit to Ruleset Notebook's own tiny hand-written `TermParser`. |
| **Tom** (INRIA) | Adds pattern-matching and rewriting constructs as a language extension embedded in Java (and formerly C). | Closest *engineering* precedent for `compiler-plan.md`: Tom compiles high-level match/rewrite constructs down to plain Java/C, which is exactly the "rules -> generated host-language code" move being planned here, just for a mainstream host language. Worth skimming Tom's papers for how they represent matching compactly in the target language. |
| **egg** (Willsey, Nandi, Wang, Flanagan, Tatlock, Panchekha - POPL 2021, "egg: Fast and Extensible Equality Saturation") | A Rust library implementing e-graphs and *equality saturation*: instead of picking one rewrite path, apply all rules simultaneously and keep every equivalent form in a compact shared structure (an e-graph), then extract the best term by some cost function at the end. | The most important modern reading here. It directly answers the "which redex do I pick, and do I lose information by committing early" question that ordered/deterministic rewriting (what Ruleset Notebook does) answers by fiat. Even if Ruleset Notebook stays deterministic for v1, understanding equality saturation reframes "term rewriting" as "term rewriting is one point in a design space," which is useful context. `egglog` (Zhang et al., PLDI 2023) merges e-graphs with Datalog and is the natural next paper after `egg` itself. |
| **Cranelift's e-graph-based optimizer** (part of the Wasmtime/Cranelift compiler backend) | A production compiler (not a research prototype) using egg-style e-graphs for instruction selection and optimization. | Good evidence that equality-saturation-flavored rewriting is now a mainstream compiler-engineering technique, not just academic - relevant if Ruleset Notebook's C-codegen experiment ever wants to justify going beyond "one deterministic pass." |
| **Coq's / Isabelle's simplifier ("simp set")** | Interactive theorem provers ship a built-in conditional term rewriter (rewrite using a curated set of lemmas as left-to-right rules) as one of their most-used automation tactics. | A good real-world example of *conditional* rewriting (Section 1) at scale, including how these tools detect and warn about non-terminating or looping simp sets - relevant prior art if Ruleset Notebook ever adds "this ruleset looks like it won't terminate" diagnostics. |

## 5. Termination and confluence tooling (for context, not near-term work)

- **AProVE** - an automated termination (and complexity) prover for term
  rewriting systems and several programming languages; competes annually in
  the **Termination Competition (TermComp)**.
- **TTT2 (Tyrolean Termination Tool 2)** - another automated termination
  prover, notable for producing checkable certificates of its proofs.
- **CoCo (Confluence Competition)** - the confluence analogue of TermComp;
  tools here automatically search for critical pairs and try to discharge
  joinability, i.e. automate exactly the Knuth–Bendix-style checking
  described in Section 2.
- **RTA / FSCD (Formal Structures for Computation and Deduction)** - the
  main academic venue for term rewriting research; FSCD is RTA merged with
  TLCA (typed lambda calculus). Good place to search for recent work if a
  specific technique (e.g. AC matching algorithms, strategy languages) needs
  a deeper dive later.

None of this tooling is a near-term dependency - `plan.md` already
deliberately defers proving termination or confluence for v1. Listed here so
"is this ruleset well-behaved?" has a known research trail to follow if the
project ever wants it.

## 6. How this connects back to Ruleset Notebook's actual decisions

- The **ordered-rules, first-match-wins, step-limited** design in `plan.md`
  is a pragmatic substitute for confluence/termination proofs: instead of
  guaranteeing a unique normal form mathematically, the tool guarantees
  *reproducibility* (same rules + same input always produce the same trace,
  because the strategy is fully deterministic) and *safety* (a step limit
  bounds runaway rewriting). That's a legitimate, well-precedented design
  point - Maude's "equations" and most simplifier tactics in proof
  assistants make the same trade for the same reason.
  See [[compiler-plan]] Section 11 for the concrete inconsistency this
  raises in the current code (`evaluate()` vs. `evaluate_with_trace()` use
  different strategies) that should be resolved before compiling either one
  to C.
- The **guard** feature (`when ?y > 0`) is a deliberately tiny slice of
  conditional rewriting (Section 1) - restricted to variable-vs-literal
  comparisons specifically so it never needs to recursively invoke the
  rewriter or execute arbitrary code to check a condition, per `plan.md`'s
  "Guards use a restricted parser and never execute arbitrary Python."
- If Ruleset Notebook ever wants a "try all rewrite orders, not just one"
  exploration mode (mentioned as future/deferred work - "branching
  exploration" in `README.md`'s roadmap and `plan.md`'s deferred list),
  equality saturation / e-graphs (Section 4, `egg`) is the modern, practical
  way to do that without an exponential blowup from naively trying every
  order.
